Spiral Galaxies

Spiral galaxies are galaxies with a bright centre and long, curved arms that spiral outwards. These arms are made up of stars, gas, and dust, and they are where many new stars are formed. Spiral galaxies are special because they have a clear, beautiful spiral shape and are among the most common types of galaxies in the universe.

Spiral galaxies are enormous, often measuring between 50,000 and 200,000 light-years across. They can contain anywhere from 100 billion to over 1 trillion stars, along with planets, nebulae, and black holes. They also rotate, with the stars and spiral arms slowly moving around the galaxy's centre over millions of years.

One famous example of a spiral galaxy is the Milky Way, which is the galaxy that contains our Solar System. Another well-known example is the Andromeda Galaxy, the closest large galaxy to the Milky Way.
